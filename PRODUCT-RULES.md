# Fanded Product Rules

These are the 10 rules that filter every product decision. Derived from Travis's founding vision.

---

## 1. The fan chose the artist — not the platform.
Every screen should remind the fan why they're here: because this artist moved them. The platform is invisible. The artist is the experience.

## 2. Voice is the primary format.
Artists speak, they don't type marketing copy. A raw voice note from the artist is worth more than a polished newsletter. Build for the voice first, text second.

## 3. AI is infrastructure, never identity.
MAWD processes, summarizes, polishes — but the fan never sees "AI-generated." The artist's voice and words are the product. AI is the plumbing.

## 4. The artist should never feel like they're working.
If it feels like a content calendar, we failed. The journal exists so the artist can speak naturally for 60 seconds and walk away. MAWD handles everything between raw thought and delivered intimacy.

## 5. Less is always more on the fan side.
Fans don't need dashboards. They need: the latest thing from their artist, a way to respond, and proof they belong. Three things. That's it.

## 6. The relationship compounds over time.
Membership number, gem count, streak, collection — these are artifacts of loyalty that grow. A fan who's been here 2 years should feel that in the product. Serial numbers don't lie.

## 7. Every dollar the fan spends should feel like it went directly to the artist.
No "platform takes 30%" energy. Fanded is the infrastructure that makes the artist's business work. The fan's money goes to the artist. Period.

## 8. MAWD is a chief of staff, not a content machine.
MAWD briefs the artist, surfaces what matters, suggests when the moment is right — like an encouraging friend who happens to know all the data. MAWD never nudges, never guilt-trips, never pushes to post.

## 9. Drops are special because they're rare.
The 48-hour spotlight exists to create urgency — but only when the artist chooses it. If everything is a drop, nothing is. The default is a letter. A drop is an event.

## 10. The product should feel like a letter from a friend, not an app.
Typography, tone, pacing — everything should feel personal, warm, and intimate. No SaaS patterns. No growth hacking. No engagement metrics visible to fans. Just the artist and the people who care.
